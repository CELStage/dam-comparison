File Organization Methods

How each DAM structures, categorizes, and surfaces your assets. The organizational model is one of the most important differences between platforms because it shapes how your team finds and works with files every day.

Why This Matters

Every DAM organizes files differently, and that difference affects everything: how fast your team finds what they need, how much manual work goes into maintaining the library, how easy it is for new team members to learn the system, and how well the platform scales as your asset count grows.

There are two broad philosophies in the DAM world. Folder-based systems mirror the file cabinet model everyone already knows. Assets live in a specific place, and you browse to that place to find them. Metadata-driven systems (sometimes called "folderless" or "flat" systems) treat every asset as a searchable record. Instead of navigating to a folder, you search, filter, and tag your way to the right file.

Most modern DAMs blend both approaches to some degree, but each platform leans heavily one direction. Understanding where each one falls on that spectrum is critical to choosing the right fit.

Canto

Folder + Album Hierarchy

📁 How It Works

Canto uses a traditional folder and album structure as its primary organizational backbone. Folders contain albums and sub-folders, while albums hold the actual files. You cannot drop files directly into a folder; they must live inside an album. Think of folders as filing cabinets and albums as the individual drawers.

📑 Structure Breakdown

  • Folders group albums and other folders into logical categories (by department, project, campaign, year)
  • Albums hold the actual asset files and live inside folders
  • Keywords are broad, admin-controlled tags (12-20 recommended) applied across the library for high-level filtering
  • Tags are contributor-level labels that add granular detail beneath keywords
  • Custom metadata fields capture organization-specific data (project codes, usage rights, expiration dates)
  • Smart Tags use AI to auto-classify images by visual content without manual input

🔎 How You Find Files

Day-to-day, users can either browse the folder tree or use the search bar. Canto's search scans file names, descriptions, keywords, tags, and custom metadata. AI Visual Search also lets you describe what you need in plain language and surface matching results, even for untagged assets. The platform recommends keeping folders lean (no more than 20-30 albums per folder) and relying on search for anything beyond that.

Real-World Analogy

A well-organized physical library. Books are shelved in sections and categories (folders/albums), but you can also use the card catalog (search + keywords) to find exactly what you need without walking every aisle.

Bynder

Flat Metadata + Smart Filters

📁 How It Works

Bynder takes a fundamentally different approach: it does not use folders at all. Every asset lives in a single, flat "Asset Bank." Organization is entirely driven by metadata, custom properties (called "metaproperties"), and search. There are no nested directories to click through. Instead, users rely on filters, tags, and natural language search to surface the right assets instantly.

📑 Structure Breakdown

  • Asset Bank is the single pool where all assets reside, regardless of type or purpose
  • Metaproperties are custom fields (text, dropdown, date, multi-select) that describe each asset's attributes
  • Smart Filters are dropdown menus at the top of the Asset Bank that expose metaproperties for one-click filtering, with asset counts next to each option
  • Dependent Metaproperties create cascading filters (e.g., selecting "North America" narrows the next filter to U.S. states)
  • Collections are flat groupings (no nesting) used to curate sets of assets for specific campaigns or teams
  • AI Agents auto-tag and enforce brand rules across the entire library

🔎 How You Find Files

Users start with the search bar or the Smart Filters at the top of the screen. There is no folder tree to browse. Bynder's search scans titles, descriptions, tags, collection names, and even text within PDF documents. Natural language processing allows queries like "outdoor photos from Q3 2025" rather than exact keyword matches. Dependent filters guide users through narrowing results step by step.

Real-World Analogy

A search engine like Google. There are no "folders" on the internet. You type what you want, apply filters, and the system surfaces the best results based on metadata and relevance, not where the file physically sits.

Brandfolder

Flat Taxonomy with Visual Sections

📁 How It Works

Brandfolder uses a flat file taxonomy that avoids traditional folder nesting, but adds a layer of visual structure through Sections, Collections, and Labels. The experience is closer to browsing a curated storefront than digging through a file system. Assets are organized visually across the top of the screen (Sections) and down the left side (Labels), with search and filters layered on top.

📑 Structure Breakdown

  • Brandfolders are the top-level containers, typically one per brand, product line, or major initiative
  • Sections (tabs across the top) group assets by type or category, e.g., "Product Shots," "Logos," "Event Images" (up to 75 sections, 10,000 assets each)
  • Labels (sidebar) provide additional categorization beneath sections for more refined browsing
  • Collections curate specific sets of assets across sections for campaigns or stakeholder groups
  • Pins are admin-created shortcuts for frequently used search terms, displayed above the search bar
  • Asset Automation Engine applies if/then rules to auto-organize new uploads based on file type, metadata, or origin

🔎 How You Find Files

Users typically browse Sections and Labels for day-to-day discovery, then refine with search and advanced filters (tags, file types, custom fields, orientation, upload date). The combination of visual browsing and search makes the system approachable for both power users and casual visitors. Duplicate detection and similarity search help surface related assets automatically.

Real-World Analogy

An online retail store. You see product categories across the top (Sections), filter by brand or attribute on the left sidebar (Labels), and can always search for exactly what you need. Assets are organized for browsing, not buried in folder trees.

OpenAsset

Project-Based Taxonomy

📁 How It Works

OpenAsset organizes files around projects, not folders or flat metadata. Every photo, document, and credential is linked to a specific project record (and often to employees and locations as well). This project-centric model mirrors how AEC firms actually think about their work, making it intuitive for architects, engineers, and marketing coordinators who already organize their thinking around project names and numbers.

📑 Structure Breakdown

  • Projects are the primary containers, each representing a real-world job, building, or engagement
  • Project Keywords are "universal labels" (market sector, service type, location) that attach to the project and automatically cascade to every file within it
  • File Keywords describe individual assets ("interior," "exterior," "rendering," "aerial") and are layered on top of project-level metadata
  • File Categories separate assets by type (photographs, documents, proposals, resumes), each with its own taxonomy
  • Employee Profiles link people to projects, certifications, and skills, creating a cross-referenced network
  • ERP Sync pulls project data from Deltek, Unanet, or Procore automatically, reducing manual entry

🔎 How You Find Files

Over 90% of searches in OpenAsset start with a project name or project keyword. Users search "Lincoln High School" or filter by "K-12 Education" and immediately see every file linked to that project. From there, file keywords and categories narrow results. Because project keywords cascade to all files within them, tagging 300 projects is far more efficient than individually tagging 15,000 files.

Real-World Analogy

A project filing room. Each drawer is labeled with a project name and number. Inside, you find every document, photo, and credential connected to that job. You do not organize by file type first; you organize by the work it belongs to.

Methodology Pros and Cons

Each organizational philosophy has trade-offs. Here is an honest breakdown of what you gain and what you give up with each approach.

Folder + Album Hierarchy

Used by: Canto
Advantages
  • Instantly familiar to anyone who has used a computer, minimal training required
  • Mirrors how most teams already think about organizing files (by department, project, year)
  • Easy to set up quickly, especially for small-to-mid-size libraries
  • Visual browsing gives a clear sense of "where everything lives"
  • Keyword and AI tagging layers add search power on top of the folder backbone
Trade-offs
  • Assets can only exist in one folder location, creating potential duplication if needed elsewhere
  • Deep folder trees become hard to navigate as the library grows into the thousands
  • Restructuring folders later is labor-intensive and disruptive
  • Teams may default to browsing instead of searching, slowing retrieval as the library scales
  • Folder organization depends on consistent naming conventions across contributors

Flat Metadata + Smart Filters

Used by: Bynder
Advantages
  • No folder limits: one asset can surface in unlimited contexts without duplication
  • Scales gracefully, a library of 5,000 or 500,000 assets searches the same way
  • Smart Filters with counts give instant visibility into what is available
  • Dependent metaproperties guide users through logical narrowing (region > country > city)
  • Supports advanced automation, headless delivery, and API-driven workflows
Trade-offs
  • Higher upfront investment: requires building a thorough metaproperty taxonomy before launch
  • Less intuitive for non-technical users who expect to see folders
  • Quality depends entirely on metadata discipline; poor tagging breaks the whole system
  • No visual "home base" for assets, everything is search-first
  • Ongoing governance required to keep taxonomy clean as the organization evolves

Flat Taxonomy with Visual Sections

Used by: Brandfolder
Advantages
  • Combines the visual familiarity of browsing with the flexibility of flat taxonomy
  • Sections and Labels make the library feel organized without rigid folder nesting
  • External stakeholders can navigate brand portals without training
  • Automation Engine reduces manual organization by applying rules to new uploads
  • Duplicate detection and similarity search prevent content bloat
Trade-offs
  • Section limit (75) and asset-per-section cap (10,000) may constrain very large libraries
  • Initial taxonomy and section planning is time-intensive
  • Search quality degrades without proper tagging and label discipline
  • Can feel less structured than a true folder system for teams that prefer explicit hierarchy
  • Requires buy-in from all contributors to maintain consistency

Project-Based Taxonomy

Used by: OpenAsset
Advantages
  • Perfectly mirrors how AEC firms already think about and discuss their work
  • Project keywords cascade automatically, tagging 300 projects covers 15,000+ files
  • ERP integration (Deltek, Unanet) can auto-populate project data, reducing setup burden
  • Cross-references between projects, employees, and credentials create a living knowledge base
  • Proposal generation pulls directly from the organizational structure
Trade-offs
  • Only practical for project-driven organizations, not suited for general brand marketing
  • Assets not linked to a project can feel orphaned with no natural home
  • Less intuitive for teams outside of AEC who do not think in "projects"
  • File-level keywords still require manual effort for granular discoverability
  • Smaller taxonomy ecosystem compared to general-purpose DAMs

Which Method Fits Your Team?

Choose Folder-Based (Canto) if...

Your team is used to organizing files in folders and you want that familiarity in a DAM. Best when most people will browse for assets rather than search, and when your library is small to mid-size (under 50,000 assets). Also a strong choice when you need minimal onboarding time.

Choose Metadata-Driven (Bynder) if...

You have a large or rapidly growing asset library and need it to scale without constant reorganization. Best when your team is comfortable with search-first workflows and you are willing to invest time upfront building a clean taxonomy. Ideal for teams that value flexibility over visual structure.

Choose Hybrid Visual (Brandfolder) if...

You want the best of both worlds: browsable visual organization for casual users, plus powerful search and automation for power users. Best when user adoption is a priority and you need external stakeholders to navigate the system with zero training.

Choose Project-Based (OpenAsset) if...

Your organization revolves around projects, not campaigns or product lines. Best for AEC firms where the primary question is "show me everything from this project." Especially strong when paired with an existing ERP system that can auto-sync project data.
